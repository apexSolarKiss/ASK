asymptotic system key

generative + abstract + conceptual

asymptotic system key / ASK consists of natively digital art objects (still images + motion) created by an artistic process combining variously painting, photography, drawing, digital manipulation and scripting. The process involves multiple inputs, including artistic intent, physical gesture, and selection / curation.

ASK are abstract images, juxtaposing minimal / monochrome against rainbow / vivid. They reflect what emanates from the inner depths of our visual cortex during a moment of enlightenment. Recurring themes >> patterns, textures, dreamscapes, the notion of order emerging from chaos, seriality, variations within repetition, and the color lavender.

The physical gestures and expressions in medium that are processed into ASK — paintings, drawings, sounds — are made together with my wife Teresa. Often Teresa's hand applies materials and gestures, while I photograph the colors and textures for further manipulation. Increasingly I also work with my son LeoV, incorporating his drawings into the process >> studioLeoV.

Having taught myself photography and contemporary art during my years as a gallerist dealer, I'm most drawn to abstract imagery — greatly influenced by Lyle Rexer's The Rise of Abstraction in Photography. My main influences are minimalist and conceptual artists, most notably Donald Judd and Sol LeWitt, and abstract photographers such as Barbara Kasten.

Signal + Noise▼

In The Signal and the Noise, Nate Silver explores a fundamental problem in our data-saturated world: we're overwhelmed with information, but struggle to discern what matters. We mistake noise — random fluctuations, distractions, false patterns — for signal. The more information we gather, the more confident we become, and yet, paradoxically, the less accurate our predictions.

This is the illusion Nassim Nicholas Taleb devotes his work to dismantling. His philosophy centers on a single, disruptive idea: the world is governed by randomness, far more than most people — especially experts — are willing to admit. Life is shaped not only by what is probable, but by what is possible — and possibility is wild, uneven, and deeply unfair.

In Fooled by Randomness, Taleb observes how easily we mistake luck for skill, how readily we attribute causality where there is merely correlation. We crave elegant narratives, but the real world doesn't play by our rules: it is opaque, nonlinear, and above all indifferent to our expectations.

Most data hides the tail risks — the rare but catastrophic events that dominate history. Much of what matters — revolutions, market crashes, pandemics, love, success — emerges from the extreme, not the mean. When Black Swan events strike, they catch us off guard because we built our models as though tomorrow will behave like yesterday.

The antidote is not better prediction, but humility. Not resilience, but antifragility — systems that gain from disorder. Nature evolves through chaos: forests renew through fire, ideas through failure, species through mutation. Stop optimizing for calm; design for stress, invite error, thrive on volatility.

Silver and Taleb converge in a call for probabilistic thinking and respect for uncertainty as a permanent feature of the human condition. What matters most lies not in the mean, but in the tails — the outlier that rewrites the narrative. To live wisely is not to impose order, but to let order emerge from chaos. Not to master uncertainty, but to dance with it.

Like Art // conceptual▼

The point of Like Art is to confront the question of whether this constitutes, like, art… or not. The Facebook page that conveys it contains no posted images or status updates — only its creation event and a statement of purpose: "The sole purpose of this Facebook page is to convey an artwork consisting of the set of users that 'like' it."

Deceptively simplistic, this almost non-page belies its complexity. The boundary of art moved from the representational toward abstraction, then toward conceptual art defined by intent rather than the hand of the artist, and finally from a pure author toward involving the receiver through relational aesthetics. Like Art is a limit case: a non-object-based, conceptual artwork whose creation is perpetual and participatory — the artwork is the set of users who 'like' it. Made in 2012, it probes modes of social-media interaction that had not yet been deeply reflected in art. Inspired by John Baldessari, Tino Sehgal, and Lawrence Weiner's Declaration of Intent.
